There is a lot of discussion today about how to evaluate and assess information. We're told that media outlets are flooding the air waves with fake news. Stave Bannon famously coined the strategy "[flood the zone][flood_the_zone]", extending the flood metaphor to a new level of crassness. The United States president, Donald Trump (formerly advised by Bannon), is caught in lies that stream out of his mouth by the hour if not minute. Yet, if you tune into Fox News you might get the impression that Donald Trump has the veracity of a choir boy.

What's certain is that we are living in an Information Age in which news can circle the globe in milliseconds. The bombardment is constant for anyone with a smart phone, computer, dish t.v., smart watch, YouTube, Facebook and / or Instagram habit and on and on.

So what is one to do? How do we go about ascertaining what to believe? Or maybe the question is how to we determine what not to believe? What sort of bedrock can we establish that anchors our own opinions and beliefs. We start with a simple technique that can be used to at least decide whether or not to continue reading a news story:

> Read between the lines.

Let's start with a simple example from the Wall Street Journal.

![Trump extends Iran ceasefire]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/posts/reading-between-the-lines/wsj_2026-04-21.png)

The first sentence of this articlet states:

> President Trump said the U.S. will extend its cease-fire with Iran and continuet the blockade of the country's ports until its leaders present a "unified proposal".

Let's analyze this first sentence and try to read between the lines. Part one states that the U.S. will extend its cease-fire with Iran. Part two goes on to state that the U.S. will continue the blockade it has imposed upon Iran's ports. Without reading further in this article we can easily spot a contradiction.

Leaving aside whether you trust anything that comes out of Donald Trump's mouth, a cease-fire is incompatible with a simultaneous naval blockade. It is well established that a [naval blockade][naval_blockade] is an act of war. Ergo, you cannot have a legitimate cease-fire while continuing with an act of war. Should we keep reading? I chose not to. 

Some might argue that Iran has instituted its own restrictions on ship traffic through the Strait of Hormuz. This is true, but Iran only imposed new rules of passage in response to the illegal and unprovoked acts of war carried out by the U.S. and Iran. These acts of war have caused immense damage to civilian infrastructure, not to mention loss of life and injury. In response, Iran chose to gate shipping transit and collect fees particularly from Gulf State countries that have provided military bases to the U.S.

What's the takeaway? We can begin to evaluate news by first looking for logical inconsistencies. The greater and more frequent the logical inconsistencies observed will naturally lead the reader toward a very healthy skepticism. Personally, when I observe logical inconsistencies that are not resolved I tend to move on to another information source.
